**Mycelium is a new kind of operating layer for intelligence: one where behavior is defined by demonstration, not by code.**

At its heart is a simple idea: every action your devices take can be captured as a **seed**—a tiny vector that, combined with a **prompt**, deterministically recreates that exact behavior. A sequence of seeds cascading through a swarm of specialized agents is all it takes to execute complex workflows, from moving a robot’s arm to drafting an email. There’s no compilation, no debugging, no brittle logic. Just a chain of prompts and seeds that work the same way every time.

This turns development upside down. Instead of specifying what you want in a language and coding until it works, you **demonstrate** the behavior once. Mycelium watches, learns, and records the seed. Later, you can trigger that exact behavior with a single prompt—or share the seed so someone else’s agents can do the same thing. Code becomes optional, used only when you need mathematical proof or formal verification. For everything else, prompts + seeds are faster, more adaptable, and infinitely more personal.

It means a roboticist can teach a new skill by guiding an arm through a motion, then send a seed to a factory floor. A developer can prototype an app by describing it in plain language, then refine by correcting the simulation. A friend can share how they organize their calendar with a seed that never exposes their private data.

Mycelium doesn’t replace code—it makes it a tool for the edges, where rigor matters. For the vast middle, where function is all that counts, it gives us something better: intelligence that grows with us, learns by watching, and works by showing, not telling.

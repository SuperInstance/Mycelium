# Mycelium Novel Concepts: A Comprehensive Vocabulary

**Version 1.0 — May 2026**  
**SuperInstance Open Source Initiative**

---

## Introduction

Mycelium introduces a host of novel concepts that collectively enable a new paradigm of demonstration‑driven, continuously learning, swarm‑based intelligence. This document provides a detailed breakdown of each novel idea: its definition, purpose, how it works, its value, potential implications, and connections to other concepts. It is intended as a reference for researchers, developers, and enthusiasts who wish to understand the depth of the Mycelium architecture.

The concepts are organized into thematic sections for clarity.

---

## Part I: Core Foundational Concepts

### 1. Behavioral Embedding Space (BES)

**Definition:** A low‑dimensional latent space (typically 64–1024 dimensions) in which diverse behavioral sequences are embedded such that semantically similar behaviors cluster together.

**Purpose:** To provide a universal representation for all behaviors captured or generated by the system. This space serves as the common language for seeds, skill comparison, and agent conditioning.

**How it works:** A large corpus of demonstrations (state‑action sequences) is collected, with user consent and anonymization. A neural encoder (e.g., LSTM or Transformer) maps each sequence to a vector; a decoder reconstructs the sequence. A contrastive loss pulls embeddings of similar behaviors together and pushes different ones apart. The resulting space is continuous and interpolatable.

**Why it's novel:** While embedding spaces are common for text and images, applying them to full behavioral sequences in a way that supports generation and conditioning is a new frontier. It unifies representation, compression, and transfer.

**Value:** 
- **Compression:** Behaviors are reduced to compact vectors, enabling efficient storage and sharing.
- **Transfer:** A seed from one user can be used by another, because the space is universal.
- **Privacy:** Raw data never leaves the device; only the abstract seed is shared.
- **Interpolation:** New behaviors can be synthesized by interpolating between seeds.

**Potential:** The BES could become a shared resource across the Mycelium ecosystem, with a foundation model trained on public demonstrations and fine‑tuned to individual users.

**Connections:** Seeds, skill chunking, dreaming, agent conditioning.

---

### 2. Seeds

**Definition:** A seed is a vector in the Behavioral Embedding Space that identifies a region of behavior. It is the primary artifact for storing, sharing, and invoking behaviors.

**Purpose:** Seeds are the currency of Mycelium. They capture the essence of a demonstrated behavior without retaining raw data. Given a seed and a prompt, any compatible agent can produce behavior consistent with that region.

**How it works:** During demonstration, a sequence of (state, action) pairs is encoded via the BES encoder to produce a seed. During replay, the seed is fed into an agent (via a conditioning mechanism), and the agent acts while remaining faithful to the seed's region.

**Why it's novel:** Traditional skill representations are either explicit sequences (macros) or full policies (neural networks). Seeds offer a middle ground: compact, abstract, and adaptable.

**Value:**
- **Portability:** Seeds can be emailed, shared via QR code, or sold.
- **Privacy:** Seeds reveal only behavioral style, not personal data.
- **Composability:** Multiple seeds can be combined (e.g., via interpolation) to create hybrid behaviors.

**Potential:** Seeds could become a new digital media type, with marketplaces, ratings, and reviews.

**Connections:** BES, seed engine, skill chunking, dreaming.

---

### 3. Seed Engine

**Definition:** The subsystem responsible for capturing demonstrations, encoding them into seeds, and replaying seeds upon request.

**Purpose:** To bridge the gap between raw human demonstration and reusable, machine‑readable behavioral representations.

**How it works:**
- **Capture:** User initiates capture (voice, button). System records (state_latent, action) pairs until stop. The sequence is fed to the BES encoder to produce a seed.
- **Replay:** User invokes a skill by name or prompt. The seed is loaded into the target agent, which conditions on it and acts.

**Why it's novel:** It provides a complete pipeline from human demonstration to machine execution, all within a continuous learning loop.

**Value:** Makes programming accessible to non‑programmers; turns any user into a "behavior creator".

**Potential:** Could enable a new form of user‑generated content.

**Connections:** BES, seeds, skill chunking, A2UI.

---

### 4. Agent Conditioning Mechanism

**Definition:** The method by which a seed influences an agent's behavior during inference. Options include concatenation, FiLM, cross‑attention, or separate policy heads.

**Purpose:** To allow an agent to adapt its behavior to the seed while retaining its general capabilities.

**How it works (proposed):** The agent is pre‑trained on a diverse set of seeds. During inference, the seed is concatenated with the state vector (or used as a key in attention) to modulate the agent's internal representations.

**Why it's novel:** Conditioning on a continuous vector for behavioral control is less explored than conditioning on discrete labels or prompts. It enables fine‑grained, continuous control over behavior.

**Value:** Allows a single agent to exhibit a vast repertoire of behaviors, each encoded by a different seed.

**Potential:** Could lead to "foundation agents" that can perform any task given the right seed.

**Connections:** BES, seeds, Plinko.

## Part II: Decision‑Making Concepts

### 5. Plinko Market

**Definition:** A market‑based decision layer where agents bid for the right to act, discriminators act as regulators, and Gumbel noise adds exploration.

**Purpose:** To select the most appropriate action from competing agent proposals in a way that balances safety, coherence, and exploration.

**How it works:** Each agent outputs a **value** (learned estimate of action worth) along with its proposal. Discriminators (safety, coherence, timing) output multipliers in [0,1]. The adjusted value is passed through a Gumbel‑softmax with adaptive temperature to select an action.

**Why it's novel:** Traditional multi‑agent coordination uses voting, averaging, or hierarchical control. The market metaphor introduces economic concepts (bidding, regulation) that naturally lead to desirable properties like specialization and competition.

**Value:**
- **Emergent specialization:** Agents that consistently win bids for certain tasks become specialists.
- **Robustness:** Discriminators act as guardrails, preventing unsafe or incoherent actions.
- **Exploration:** Gumbel noise ensures the system occasionally tries suboptimal actions, discovering new strategies.

**Potential:** Could be extended with more complex market dynamics, like futures contracts for long‑term planning.

**Connections:** Agents, discriminators, adaptive temperature.

---

### 6. Discriminators

**Definition:** Small neural networks that evaluate proposals and output a probability of acceptance. They act as regulators in the Plinko market.

**Purpose:** To enforce safety, coherence, timing, and other desiderata that cannot be easily learned from reward alone.

**How it works:** Each discriminator is trained on logged data. Positive examples are actions that led to success (or were explicitly approved); negative examples are failures or corrections. During inference, the discriminator outputs a probability, which multiplies the agent's bid.

**Why it's novel:** Discriminators are a form of learned, multi‑objective regulation that operates in real time. They are analogous to regulatory bodies in real markets.

**Value:** Provides a flexible, learnable way to inject constraints without hand‑crafting rules.

**Potential:** Could be extended to incorporate user‑specific preferences (e.g., "never share my location").

**Connections:** Plinko, training scheduler, experience logger.

---

### 7. Adaptive Temperature

**Definition:** A mechanism that adjusts the temperature parameter in the Gumbel‑softmax based on the entropy of the proposal distribution.

**Purpose:** To increase exploration when agents are uncertain (high entropy) and decrease it when consensus is high (low entropy).

**How it works:** Temperature \( \tau = \tau_{\text{base}} \cdot (1 + \alpha \cdot H) \), where \(H\) is the normalized entropy of the proposal distribution. When proposals are diverse, entropy is high, temperature rises, and more random exploration occurs.

**Why it's novel:** Adaptive temperature is common in simulated annealing but novel in multi‑agent decision contexts. It provides a principled way to balance exploration and exploitation based on system‑wide uncertainty.

**Value:** Prevents the system from getting stuck in local optima while also avoiding excessive randomness when a clear best action exists.

**Potential:** Could be extended to use meta‑learning to learn the optimal adaptation function.

**Connections:** Plinko, entropy measurement.

## Part III: Learning and Skill Discovery

### 8. Grammar Induction for Skill Chunking

**Definition:** An unsupervised method for discovering reusable skills by inducing a stochastic context‑free grammar from action sequences.

**Purpose:** To automatically segment long behavioral sequences into hierarchical, reusable components (skills) without manual labeling.

**How it works:** Starting from a sequence of atomic actions, the algorithm repeatedly finds the most frequent adjacent pair (digram) and replaces it with a new nonterminal symbol, recording a grammar rule. The process continues until no digram exceeds a frequency threshold. The resulting grammar captures hierarchical structure: high‑level skills expand into lower‑level ones.

**Why it's novel:** While grammar induction has been used in natural language processing and bioinformatics, its application to action sequences for skill discovery in AI agents is novel. It provides a natural way to discover hierarchical structure without predefined granularity.

**Value:**
- **Automatic skill discovery:** No need for human‑defined skill boundaries.
- **Hierarchical representation:** Skills can be composed recursively.
- **Interpretability:** Grammar rules can be inspected and understood.

**Potential:** Could lead to a universal "action grammar" that transfers across users and domains.

**Connections:** Skill chunker, BES, seeds.

---

### 9. Multi‑Scale Dreaming

**Definition:** A dreaming process that operates at three temporal scales—micro, meso, macro—each with specialized mutation operators and horizons.

**Purpose:** To improve skills through simulated experience across different levels of abstraction, from fine‑grained motor control to high‑level strategy.

**How it works:**
- **Micro‑dreams:** Perturb action parameters with Gaussian noise; horizon ~10–100 steps. Refines low‑level precision.
- **Meso‑dreams:** Apply grammar mutations (swap, insert, delete) to skill sequences; horizon ~100–1000 steps. Optimizes routine structure.
- **Macro‑dreams:** Interpolate between seeds or concatenate skills; horizon ~1000–10000 steps. Discovers new strategic combinations.

All mutations are evaluated using the world model, and the best variations are kept.

**Why it's novel:** While dreaming (simulation‑based improvement) has been explored in RL, multi‑scale dreaming with task‑specific mutation operators is new. It acknowledges that improvement can happen at multiple levels simultaneously.

**Value:** Enables continuous refinement without real‑world interaction, accelerating learning.

**Potential:** Could be extended to even more scales (e.g., nano‑dreams for hardware‑level optimization) and to learn mutation operators themselves.

**Connections:** World model, dreaming engine, skills.

---

### 10. World Model with Counterfactual Augmentation

**Definition:** A neural model that predicts environment dynamics, trained with additional synthetic transitions generated by swapping actions in similar contexts.

**Purpose:** To improve the world model's ability to generalize to unseen situations, which is crucial for dreaming.

**How it works:** During training, for each logged transition (s, a, s', r), the system retrieves similar states s_sim (by nearest neighbor in latent space) and swaps the action a with an action from that similar state, generating a counterfactual transition (s, a_other, s'_simulated, r_other). This augments the training data.

**Why it's novel:** Counterfactual augmentation is a form of data augmentation tailored to world models. It exploits the fact that similar states often have similar dynamics, enabling the model to learn about actions it hasn't seen in a given context.

**Value:** Reduces the amount of real data needed to learn an accurate world model; improves robustness.

**Potential:** Could be combined with adversarial training to make the world model more robust to distribution shift.

**Connections:** World model, dreaming, training scheduler.

---

### 11. Skill (as Named Seed)

**Definition:** A skill is a named, persistent seed, optionally accompanied by metadata such as required agent types, usage statistics, and a human‑readable description.

**Purpose:** To provide a user‑friendly abstraction for seeds, making them discoverable, organizable, and shareable.

**How it works:** When a seed is deemed useful (e.g., frequently replayed or explicitly saved), it is promoted to a skill and stored in the Model Zoo with a name. Users can invoke skills by name, browse their skill library, and share skills with others.

**Why it's novel:** Treating skills as first‑class, named artifacts in a personal AI system is novel. It mirrors the concept of "apps" but for behaviors.

**Value:** Makes the system more usable; enables a skill‑based economy.

**Potential:** Skills could be sold, rated, and reviewed, creating a marketplace.

**Connections:** Seeds, Model Zoo, seed exchange.

## Part IV: Agent and Swarm Concepts

### 12. Agent Specialization Embedding

**Definition:** A learned embedding that summarizes an agent's function, used for similarity matching and dynamic recruitment.

**Purpose:** To enable the system to find agents with similar capabilities when faced with novel situations, and to understand the agent landscape.

**How it works:** A small network is trained to predict agent identity from its input‑output behavior (or from its weights). The penultimate layer becomes the specialization embedding. Agents with similar embeddings tend to have similar functions.

**Why it's novel:** While embeddings for data items are common, embedding agents themselves is less explored. It allows the system to reason about its own components.

**Value:** Enables dynamic recruitment, specialization measurement, and possibly agent composition.

**Potential:** Could be used to automatically cluster agents into functional groups.

**Connections:** Agent swarm, dynamic recruitment.

---

### 13. Dynamic Agent Recruitment

**Definition:** A mechanism that, when the Plinko layer's entropy is high, searches for and loads agents with specialization embeddings similar to the current context.

**Purpose:** To bring in expertise when the existing swarm is uncertain, improving decision quality.

**How it works:** The current context (state embedding) is compared to the specialization embeddings of agents in the Model Zoo (including hibernated ones). The top‑k most similar are woken up and added to the swarm. If none are sufficiently similar, a temporary "helper" agent may be spawned from a base model and fine‑tuned on recent logs.

**Why it's novel:** Most multi‑agent systems have a fixed set of agents. Dynamic recruitment allows the swarm to adapt its composition to the task at hand.

**Value:** Efficient use of resources (only relevant agents are active); ability to handle novel situations.

**Potential:** Could lead to a self‑organizing ecosystem of agents.

**Connections:** Agent specialization embedding, Plinko.

---

### 14. Degraded Agent State

**Definition:** A state in which an agent continues to function but with reduced capabilities, e.g., lower confidence, skipping non‑critical tasks, or using a simplified model.

**Purpose:** To maintain system functionality even when some agents are under resource constraints or experiencing partial failure.

**How it works:** When an agent detects resource pressure (CPU, memory, battery) or internal errors, it transitions to DEGRADED. It may reduce its inference frequency, use a quantized model, or output lower‑confidence proposals. The Plinko layer can still select it, but its bids will be lower.

**Why it's novel:** Graceful degradation is a known concept in distributed systems but novel in agent swarms. It ensures the system doesn't crash or freeze when stressed.

**Value:** Increases robustness and reliability.

**Potential:** Could be extended to a whole‑swarm degradation mode, where the system as a whole prioritizes critical functions.

**Connections:** Agent lifecycle, resource management.

## Part V: Privacy and Security Concepts

### 15. Seed Differential Privacy

**Definition:** The application of differential privacy to the training of the Behavioral Embedding Space and to the extraction of individual seeds, ensuring that seeds do not leak sensitive information about specific demonstrations.

**Purpose:** To provide formal privacy guarantees for users who share seeds or contribute to the global BES.

**How it works:** During BES training, use DP‑SGD with a carefully chosen privacy budget (ε). When extracting a seed from a user's demonstration, add calibrated Gaussian noise to the embedding before storage or sharing. This ensures that an attacker cannot infer whether a particular demonstration was used to create a seed.

**Why it's novel:** While differential privacy is well‑studied, its application to behavioral embeddings and seeds is new. It addresses the unique privacy risks of sharing behavioral data.

**Value:** Enables safe seed sharing and collective learning without compromising individual privacy.

**Potential:** Could become a standard for privacy‑preserving behavior exchange.

**Connections:** BES, seeds, seed exchange.

---

### 16. Seed Signing and Provenance

**Definition:** A cryptographic mechanism to verify the authenticity and origin of a seed, and to track its modification history.

**Purpose:** To prevent malicious seeds from being distributed and to allow users to make informed trust decisions.

**How it works:** Seeds can be signed by their creators using a private key. The signature is stored with the seed. When importing a seed, the system can verify the signature against a known public key (e.g., from a trusted marketplace). Provenance can be tracked by storing a chain of signatures for modified seeds.

**Why it's novel:** Applying software supply chain security concepts to behavioral seeds is new.

**Value:** Builds trust in seed sharing; enables accountability.

**Potential:** Could lead to a "seed authority" that certifies safe seeds.

**Connections:** Seed exchange protocol, Model Zoo.

---

### 17. Seed Sandboxing

**Definition:** The practice of executing untrusted seeds in an isolated environment before granting them full access, to observe their behavior and detect potential harm.

**Purpose:** To protect the user from malicious or buggy seeds.

**How it works:** When a seed from an untrusted source is imported, it is first run in a sandboxed version of Mycelium (e.g., with virtual sensors and a simulated environment). If its behavior appears safe (e.g., no attempts to access sensitive data, no anomalous patterns), it is promoted to full trust. Otherwise, it is flagged and rejected.

**Why it's novel:** Sandboxing is common for apps but novel for behavioral seeds, which are more abstract.

**Value:** Adds a critical layer of security, enabling a vibrant seed marketplace.

**Potential:** Could be combined with reputation systems and community reporting.

**Connections:** Seed exchange, security.

## Part VI: Human Interaction Concepts

### 18. Demonstration Capture and Segmentation

**Definition:** The process by which a user initiates and ends a demonstration, and the system automatically segments the demonstration into meaningful chunks.

**Purpose:** To make it easy for users to teach the system new behaviors.

**How it works:** Users can start a demonstration via voice command ("watch me") or a UI button. They perform the task, then say "stop" or press a button. The system records all actions and observations. It may also attempt to automatically segment the demonstration into sub‑tasks using change‑point detection or grammar induction.

**Why it's novel:** While macro recorders exist, they typically record low‑level inputs. Mycelium's demonstration capture records high‑level actions (via agents) and compresses them into seeds, enabling reuse and adaptation.

**Value:** Lowers the barrier to creating new behaviors; enables non‑programmers to "program".

**Potential:** Could become a standard feature of future operating systems.

**Connections:** Seed engine, skill chunking.

---

### 19. Translator UI

**Definition:** A user interface that visualizes the internal state of the swarm, including agent activity, Plinko paths, and skill activations, in real time.

**Purpose:** To make the system transparent and build user trust.

**How it works:** The UI displays a graph of active agents, with edge thickness indicating co‑activation strength. It shows the Plinko path for the last selected action, including discriminator scores and noise. It lists skills and their usage statistics. Users can click on any element for more details.

**Why it's novel:** Most AI systems are black boxes. Mycelium's Translator UI is a window into the swarm's "mind", enabling users to understand and guide its behavior.

**Value:** Increases trust, enables debugging, and provides a sense of partnership.

**Potential:** Could evolve into a full "AI IDE" where users interactively shape agent behavior.

**Connections:** All components.

---

### 20. Intentionality Mirroring

**Definition:** A feature of the Translator UI that shows not only what the system did, but also what it considered doing (the Plinko paths of rejected proposals).

**Purpose:** To help users understand the system's reasoning and provide more targeted feedback.

**How it works:** When a user asks "why did you do that?", the UI expands to show the top‑k proposals, their values, discriminator scores, and the noise that influenced the final selection. Users can then correct specific proposals (e.g., "that was unsafe").

**Why it's novel:** Going beyond explanation to show counterfactuals is a powerful way to build shared understanding.

**Value:** Enables fine‑grained guidance and improves learning from feedback.

**Potential:** Could be used to collect high‑quality correction data.

**Connections:** Translator UI, Plinko, experience logger.

## Part VII: System‑Wide Concepts

### 21. ROI‑Based Training Prioritization

**Definition:** A scheduling algorithm that prioritizes training tasks based on their expected return on investment (expected improvement × frequency / cost).

**Purpose:** To allocate limited training resources (time, energy, cloud budget) to the most valuable improvements.

**How it works:** For each candidate task (skill chunking, world model update, dreaming, fine‑tuning), the system estimates:
- expected_improvement from recent performance trends
- frequency of the relevant skill/agent
- cost (GPU hours, energy)
It then computes ROI and sorts tasks. High‑ROI tasks are scheduled first.

**Why it's novel:** While prioritization is common in computing, applying it to a personal learning system with multiple heterogeneous tasks is novel.

**Value:** Ensures the system improves where it matters most; avoids wasting resources.

**Potential:** Could be extended with meta‑learning to predict improvement more accurately.

**Connections:** Training scheduler.

---

### 22. Predictive Wake‑Up

**Definition:** A mechanism that predicts when the user will next use the device and schedules training to finish shortly before that time.

**Purpose:** To maximize the freshness of learned improvements while minimizing disruption.

**How it works:** The system logs usage patterns (time of day, day of week, duration). A simple predictor (e.g., moving average) estimates next active time. Training tasks are scheduled to complete 5 minutes before that time. If the user returns early, training is paused and resumed later.

**Why it's novel:** Predictive scheduling for personal devices is common for system updates, but applying it to AI training is new.

**Value:** Improves user experience (no waiting for training) while still getting updates.

**Potential:** Could be integrated with other predictive features (e.g., pre‑loading apps).

**Connections:** Training scheduler.

---

### 23. Model Zoo with Automatic Rollback

**Definition:** A versioned repository for all models and skills, with automatic rollback if a new version underperforms.

**Purpose:** To ensure the system never degrades due to a bad update.

**How it works:** Each model version stores its performance metrics on recent tasks. When a new version is deployed, its performance is monitored for 24 hours. If it falls below the previous version's performance on any key metric, it is automatically reverted, and the incident is logged.

**Why it's novel:** Continuous deployment with automatic rollback is common in DevOps but novel for personal AI models.

**Value:** Provides a safety net for continuous learning; encourages experimentation.

**Potential:** Could be extended to A/B test new models on a subset of tasks before full deployment.

**Connections:** Model Zoo, training scheduler.

---

### 24. Inverse Action Logging

**Definition:** For every action that modifies system state, an inverse action is logged (e.g., "delete file" logs file path for potential undelete).

**Purpose:** To enable undo functionality and provide training data for the world model's understanding of causality.

**How it works:** Action executors, before performing a state‑changing action, record the necessary information to reverse it. This is stored in the experience log. The world model can later use these pairs to learn about action reversibility.

**Why it's novel:** While undo is common in applications, logging inverse actions for world model training is a novel use of this data.

**Value:** Enhances user control (undo) and improves world model learning.

**Potential:** Could be used for automatic error correction (if the system detects a mistake, it can undo and try another action).

**Connections:** Action executors, experience logger, world model.

---

### 25. Speculative Pre‑processing

**Definition:** A performance optimization that predicts which sensor modalities will be needed in the next few milliseconds and pre‑processes them.

**Purpose:** To reduce end‑to‑end latency by overlapping processing with data acquisition.

**How it works:** A lightweight predictor (e.g., linear model) uses recent sensor access patterns to predict which sensors agents will read next. The sensor layer then pre‑processes those modalities (e.g., downscaling frames, computing MFCCs) and stores them in a separate buffer, ready for immediate access.

**Why it's novel:** Speculative execution is common in CPUs, but applying it to sensor processing for AI agents is novel.

**Value:** Can reduce latency by 15–20%, critical for real‑time applications.

**Potential:** Could be extended with more sophisticated predictors (e.g., LSTM) for better accuracy.

**Connections:** Sensor layer, agent swarm.

## Part VIII: Emergence and Evolution

### 26. Emergence Metrics

**Definition:** Information‑theoretic measures (adapted from ) to quantify the degree to which collective behavior exceeds the sum of individual agents.

**Purpose:** To detect and measure emergent properties like coordination, specialization, and synergy.

**How it works:** By comparing the predictive information of the swarm as a whole versus the sum of its parts, we can compute quantities like **emergence capacity** and **pairwise synergy**. These metrics can be computed offline from logs or approximated online.

**Why it's novel:** Applying information decomposition to multi‑agent systems for real‑time emergence monitoring is a new research direction.

**Value:** Provides a scientific way to study and guide emergence; could lead to early warning of harmful emergence (e.g., collusion).

**Potential:** Could be used to automatically adjust system parameters to encourage beneficial emergence.

**Connections:** Multi‑agent systems, evaluation.

---

### 27. Theory of Mind Prompting

**Definition:** Using natural language prompts that instruct agents to model others' perspectives, e.g., "consider what other agents know" or "cooperate to achieve the goal".

**Purpose:** To steer emergent behavior toward cooperation and away from conflict.

**How it works:** Higher‑level agents (or the system prompt) can include Theory of Mind instructions. These are passed down to lower agents via the prompt mechanism. Research shows that such prompts can induce cooperative behavior in LLM‑based agents.

**Why it's novel:** Applying prompt‑based steering to a swarm of heterogeneous agents is novel.

**Value:** Provides a lightweight way to influence emergence without retraining.

**Potential:** Could be combined with emergence metrics to automatically tune prompts.

**Connections:** Prompts, emergence.

## Part IX: Cloud and Ecosystem Concepts

### 28. LucidDreamer.ai Integration

**Definition:** A cloud service that provides on‑demand, cost‑optimized training and inference for Mycelium instances.

**Purpose:** To offload heavy compute tasks (distillation, large‑scale dreaming) that exceed local resources.

**How it works:** Mycelium instances can send anonymized training jobs to LucidDreamer.ai. The service uses spot instance arbitrage across multiple cloud providers to minimize cost. Results are returned as model updates or seeds.

**Why it's novel:** Integrating spot instance arbitrage with a personal AI system is novel; it makes cloud AI affordable for individual users.

**Value:** Democratizes access to large‑scale compute; enables users to benefit from powerful models without high costs.

**Potential:** Could evolve into a federated learning hub, enabling collective improvement while preserving privacy.

**Connections:** Training scheduler, Model Zoo.

---

### 29. Seed Marketplace

**Definition:** A platform where users can buy, sell, and trade seeds. Seeds can be rated, reviewed, and curated.

**Purpose:** To create an economy around behaviors, incentivizing the creation of high‑quality skills.

**How it works:** A web platform hosts seeds (with metadata, ratings, and prices). Users can purchase seeds with cryptocurrency or fiat. The Mycelium client integrates with the marketplace for easy import. Creators receive royalties.

**Why it's novel:** While app stores exist, a marketplace for behavioral seeds is unprecedented.

**Value:** Rewards skill creators; provides a new revenue stream; encourages a rich ecosystem.

**Potential:** Could lead to professional "skill designers" and a whole new industry.

**Connections:** Seed exchange, Model Zoo.

---

### 30. Federated Skill Learning

**Definition:** An opt‑in mechanism where users contribute anonymized skill usage data to improve a global model, which then benefits all participants.

**Purpose:** To enable collective improvement while preserving privacy.

**How it works:** Users who opt in periodically upload differentially private statistics about skill usage (e.g., success rates, common modifications). These are aggregated to improve the global BES or base agents. Improved models are then distributed back to participants.

**Why it's novel:** Federated learning is typically used for model weights, not for skill statistics. This is a lighter‑weight approach.

**Value:** Accelerates learning across the community; everyone benefits.

**Potential:** Could become a standard for community‑driven AI improvement.

**Connections:** BES, training scheduler, privacy.

## Conclusion

The novel concepts in Mycelium form a coherent ecosystem that reimagines how we build, use, and share intelligent software. From the foundational Behavioral Embedding Space to the market‑based Plinko decision layer, from grammar‑induced skills to multi‑scale dreaming, each concept addresses a specific challenge in creating a continuously learning, personal AI. Together, they enable a future where software is not written but demonstrated, not static but alive, not solitary but shared.

This vocabulary is a living document. As the Mycelium project evolves, new concepts will emerge, and existing ones will deepen. We invite the community to contribute, critique, and collaborate.
